
## The idea

You try crossing a room, to get to the other side you must first go half way, then half of the remaining distance, then half again ... on and on until you get to the wall. However, there will always be another half remaining, mean you will never reach the wall, right? This was proposed by Zeno of Elea and his conclusion was that motion was impossible, as you can move anything as you can never escape this sequence of halves. This was known as Zeno's paradox and is displayed below, showing that you can get very close to the end wall but never truly reach it as you always have a half still to cover, shown by the decreaingly small distance in the box below.

## What it shows

By step 20, it may seem like you have reached the wall but, the distance between you and the wall is still not zero. An infinite number of steps summing to one finite distance. This stunned Zeno's contempories make infinte actions in finite space seemed impossible to thinkers of the time. 

## A Mathematicians Answer 

The Answer was found by simplifying the expression 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + ... into Sₙ = 1 − (1/2)ⁿ where n is the number of steps taken. We can see this is the same as the previous but lets us look at a more easier to grasp equation with no infitine sumations. In this equation we can see that when n nears ∞ the (1/2)ⁿ will become extremely close to 0 and therefore as n tends to infinity the sum becomes only 1. This is means that the sum converges to 1, meaning that you reach the wall. 

## A Philosophers Response

Zeno's question was never posed to be answered mathematically, but supposed to ask whether a process ever completes if it consits of infinitely sub steps, even if their total appears finite. Aristotle attempted to answer this by proposing that distances can either be potentially divisble infinitely many times or actually divisible infinitely many times. This complemented his friend Democratus who was one of the first believers of the atom - literally meaning 'uncuttable'. Aristotle beleived that nothing can be divided infinitely and therefore, even when a distance was travelled in infinite halves like Zeno's paradox, you would get to atomic distances and would not be able to continue to cut the remaining distance.
